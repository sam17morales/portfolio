Old Fashion Mexican Standoff

Western Short Story — Original Work

The desert sun blazed high overhead, casting sharp shadows across the dusty main street. The town was a rough patch of civilization carved out of the barren wilderness, its wooden buildings weathered by years of relentless heat and sandstorms. A crooked sign creaked in the breeze, hanging from the post of the general store, while the blacksmith’s forge belched lazy tendrils of smoke into the cloudless sky. A few townsfolk moved about their business, their faces drawn and sweaty, their boots stirring up small clouds of dust with every step. The occasional bark of a dog or the distant clatter of a wagon wheel broke the stillness. In the heart of the town stood the saloon, its faded facade bearing the scars of too many drunken brawls, its swinging doors a gateway to trouble.

A lone cowboy rode into town, his wide-brimmed hat tilted forward to shield his eyes from the relentless noon sun. His tattered trench coat swayed gently with the rhythm of his horse’s gait, and the glint of a revolver at his hip caught the light with each sway. The cowboy dismounted, dusted himself off, and tied his horse to the hitching post outside the saloon. He paused for a moment, his gaze sweeping over the town, before pushing through the saloon doors.

Inside, the air was thick with smoke and the scent of spilled whiskey. The room was dimly lit by a few flickering oil lamps mounted on the walls, their glow barely cutting through the haze. A long, battered bar stretched across one side, its surface scarred with knife marks and dark stains. Shelves behind the bar were lined with dusty bottles, some half-empty, others filled with mysterious amber liquids. Worn wooden tables were scattered throughout, most occupied by groups of rough looking men hunched over card games or nursing drinks. A creaky staircase led to a shadowy second floor, where faint murmurs and the occasional creak of floorboards hinted at private rooms. The piano in the corner played a jaunty tune, though the keys sounded slightly out of tune, adding to the saloon’s shabby charm.

A group of young men in tattered hats and mismatched clothes sat at a corner table, their laughter loud and grating. They were trouble, anyone could see that. The leader of the group, a beefy man with a scar running down his cheek, glanced up as the cowboy entered, his smirk spreading as he nudged one of his companions. The rest of the saloon’s patrons kept their heads down, not wanting to draw attention.

The cowboy walked to the bar, his spurred boots echoing against the wooden floor. The bartender, a wiry man with a bushy mustache, nodded as the cowboy approached.

“Whatcha having sir” said the bartender. “Whiskey, neat” mumbled the cowboy. The bartender whipped out a small glass and placed it in front of the patron and poured a plentiful glass of the liquid gold. The cowboy took the glass, raised it slightly in acknowledgment, and took a slow sip, savoring the burn as it traveled down his throat. “Well, lookie here,” the leader drawled, pushing back his chair and swaggering over to the bar. “A stranger in town. You lost, cowboy?” The cowboy didn’t respond, his gaze steady on his glass. The leader’s smirk faltered, and he stepped closer. “Hey, I’m talkin’ to you.” The cowboy raised his eyes, calm and unflinching. “I heard you.” The saloon fell silent, all eyes on the confrontation. The leader’s grin returned, though it didn’t reach his eyes. “You’ve got a smart mouth, stranger. Maybe we oughta teach you some manners.” “Maybe you oughta mind your own business,” the cowboy replied, his voice low and even. His calm demeanor only seemed to fuel the leader’s anger. The leader slammed a hand down on the bar, making the glasses rattle. “You think you’re tough, huh? Walking in here like you own the place. You don’t know who you’re messing with.” The cowboy set his glass down and turned to fully face the leader, his expression unreadable. “I know exactly who I’m dealing with,” he said. “A loudmouth who doesn’t know when to back down.”

A murmur rippled through the room as the leader’s face darkened. His gang shifted in their seats, some looking eager, others uneasy. The leader jabbed a finger at the cowboy’s chest.

“You think you’re real clever, don’t you? Well, let’s see how clever you are when I put you in the dirt.” “Leave him be, Clay,” the bartender said, his voice wavering. “He’s done nothin’ to you.” Clay ignored the bartender, his hand drifting toward the revolver on his hip. “How ’bout we settle this outside?” The cowboy’s expression didn’t change. “If that’s what you want.”

Clay’s gang whooped and hollered as they followed the two men out into the street. The townsfolk gathered to watch, peeking from behind curtains and around corners. The cowboy stood at one end of the dusty road, his trench coat billowing slightly in the evening breeze. Clay took his position at the other end, a cocky grin plastered across his face.

“You know how this goes, stranger,” Clay called out. “We’ll take ten steps, then draw.” The cowboy nodded, his face unreadable. The two men turned their backs to each other, and the onlookers held their breath as the counting began. “One... two... three...”

Clay’s hand hovered near his gun, his fingers twitching. By the sixth step, his grin twisted into something darker. At seven, he spun around, drawing his revolver and firing. The shot cracked through the air.

The cowboy had anticipated the betrayal, his own gun slipping from a hidden holster stitched into his trench coat. The cowboy fired a single shot through a small, carefully hidden hole in his coat, before Clay’s bullet could find its mark.

Clay staggered, his gun clattering to the ground as he clutched his chest. He fell to his knees, disbelief etched across his face. The cowboy holstered his weapon and walked toward the fallen man, his boots kicking up small puffs of dust with each step.

COWBOY You cheated. CLAY (gasping) And you paid the price.

Clay grasped at the air around him, until he finally slumped to the floor with the same look of disbelief etched into his face, for the rest of time.

COWBOY (to Clay’s gang) Anyone else want a shot?

Clay’s gang, seeing their leader bested, scattered like rats. The townsfolk emerged from their hiding places, murmuring among themselves. The cowboy turned without another word and strode back to his horse. He untied the reins, mounted, and tipped his hat to the stunned crowd.

As he rode off into the fading light, the saloon’s piano resumed its tune, and life in the town returned to its uneasy normalcy. But the legend of the stranger who bested Clay in a rigged standoff would live on, whispered over whiskey glasses for years to come.
